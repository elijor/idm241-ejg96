Designing and Building Micro-interactions for Urban Outfitters

1

Overview

In this case study, the focus was on the redesign and enhancement of Urban Outfitters' Quick Shop modal and associated features for class focused around design and development of micro-interactions. The primary objective was to optimize the existing micro-interaction, which included refining elements like the favorite button, size buttons, and add to cart buttons. By carefully working through the design phase, the project smoothly moved from ideas to reality. I brought the design to life, turning it into functional micro-interactions using HTML, CSS, and Javascript. Some big lessons I’ve learned from this project include analyzing and comparing what issues lie with the existing design and identifying areas I could improve upon, documenting triggers, rules, feedback, and any present loops or modes, and seamlessly integrating the design into code. The result is a more engaging user experience Quick Shop modal that aligns with Urban Outfitters' brand and customer expectations.

2

Context & Challenges

Context

Before this project, my experience with micro-interactions was limited to basic hover effects on elements within larger websites. These interactions were typically viewed as minor components rather than focal points. However, this project allowed me to delve into the significance of micro-interactions, recognizing their crucial role in enhancing user experience and helping me to understand of their importance within web design.

Timeline

This project was developed in the course of a 10 week class that met once a week. The first two classes focused on establishing what micro-interactions are and how to break them into their basic components (triggers, rules, feedback, loops & modes). During the rest of the class, alternating weeks were dedicating to describing what my project would do and the next week I’d build it out. Every week, there was usually a review and critique that would guide my overall design and development.

The Problem

Urban Outfitters' current micro-interaction exhibits a lack of smoothness, characterized by a jumpy and disjointed behavior that disrupts the user experience. The existing design fails to establish a natural flow, falling short of aligning seamlessly with the brand's ideals and overall aesthetic.

Objective

I seek to solve the stated problem by designing and building a more enjoyable and united experience. I will focus only on the quick shop modal and any of it's accompanying interactive features. Features like: the favorite button, size buttons, quantity increase or decrease buttons, and add to cart button.

3

Process

Workflow

The first step was identifying a website that has micro-interactions that could be improved upon. Once that site was established, then the existing site's micro-interactions had to be broken down into it's core pieces, it's trigger, rules, feedback, loops & modes. After that, there was a cycle of planning an improvement by describing it's core piece, reviewing and critiquing that plan, then building it out. This cycle would repeat for the remaining weeks until the project was finalized.

Development

One Modal, Three Products

One issue I've encountered during the development phase of the project was having one modal display become visible regardless of which product is displayed. To avoid having three different modals, I coded a function that would replace the current text (price and name) and image source of the modal depending on which product was selected.

Reset Selections

An error that occurred after I closed out of the modal and opened a new one was that the size and quantity still resembled what was set for the previous pop-up regardless of which product was selected so I made an adjustment that whenever a modal was closed, all the selected buttons and quantity would reset.

5

Results

I believe the project was successful but could've been improved in several areas. In the future, I'd allot more time for planning out all the features at once instead of doing a few at a time, then developing them, a few at a time. I feel that starting with a plan that encompassed all of the micro-interactions before jumping into development would've resulted in a cleaner and more finished project.

I feel that I learned a lot, including how even small movements or transitions like a quick fade can make a much larger difference. I've also realized that complex transitions and animations will likely not equate to a better experience. Making a user wait longer or have to sit through the same animation over and over will result in frustration, and eventually an aversion to use the site or product again.

Overall, I've taken away many important lessons from this class that I will implement to enhance my projects beyond basic in the future.
